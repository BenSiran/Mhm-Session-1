The Purpose: Why Do We Use It?

Present Simple tells us about things that are permanent, regular, or always true. Think of it as the tense for habits, routines, facts, and general truths.

When to Use Present Simple:

1. ROUTINES & HABITS

Things you do regularly

‚úì I usually prepare lunch before heading out.
‚úì She works at a hospital on weekends.
‚úì They always eat dinner together.

2. PERMANENT FACTS

Scientific truths, general knowledge

‚úì Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius.
‚úì The sun rises in the east.
‚úì Water doesn't boil at 80 degrees.

3. FREQUENCY EXPRESSIONS

Time markers: every day/week/month, always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never, on weekends

The Purpose: Why Do We Use It?

Past Simple describes completed actions that happened at a specific time in the past. The action is finished and disconnected from now.

  • WHEN + PAST ‚Üí when I was young, when I was a teenager
  • THIS MORNING / AFTERNOON ‚Üí (only if the time period is finished)

Age Expressions:

The Purpose: Why Do We Use It?

Past Continuous sets the background scene for another action. It shows what was in progress when something else happened.

The Story Structure:

üìå Story Pattern:
Background (Past Continuous) + Interruption (Past Simple)

Think of it like a movie:
‚Ä¢ Past Continuous = the scene that's already happening
‚Ä¢ Past Simple = the action that suddenly occurs

Two Ways to Say It:

Option 1: Using WHILE (focuses on background)

WHILE + Past Continuous, Past Simple

‚úì While I was crossing the street, a cat jumped on my head.
‚úì While I was watching TV, someone knocked on the door.
‚úì While she was cooking dinner, the phone rang.

Option 2: Using WHEN (focuses on interruption)

Past Simple + WHEN + Past Continuous

‚úì When a cat jumped on my head, I was crossing the street.
‚úì When someone knocked on the door, I was watching TV.
‚úì When the phone rang, she was cooking dinner.

More Examples:

‚úì She was cooking dinner when the power went out.
‚úì What were you doing when I called you?
‚úì While we were having breakfast, the mailman arrived.
‚úì I was walking home when it started raining.

5. PRESENT PERFECT (ÿ≠ÿßŸÑ ⁄©ÿßŸÖŸÑ)

The Purpose: Why Do We Use It?

Present Perfect connects the past with the present. It describes experiences, changes, or actions that have present relevance or unknown/unspecified timing.

Formula: have/has + past participle (V3)

I/You/We/They + have + V3
He/She/It + has + V3

Main Uses:

1. LIFE EXPERIENCES

Time is not important - just the experience itself

‚úì I have visited Paris three times.
‚úì Have you ever tried sushi?
‚úì She has never seen snow.
‚úì We have traveled to many countries.

2. CHANGES OVER TIME

From past until now

‚úì Technology has changed our lives dramatically.
‚úì My English has improved a lot this year.
‚úì The city has grown significantly.

3. RECENT ACTIONS with PRESENT RESULT

Something happened recently and we can see the result NOW

‚úì I've lost my keys. (So I can't open the door NOW)
‚úì She has just arrived. (She's here NOW)
‚úì They've finished the project. (It's complete NOW)
‚úì I've broken my phone. (It's broken NOW)

4. UNFINISHED TIME

Today, this week, this month, this year - the time period is not finished yet

‚úì I've had two meetings today. (Day isn't finished)
‚úì We've watched three movies this week. (Week continues)
‚úì She has called me five times this morning. (Morning continues)

Common Time Markers:

üìå These words often signal Present Perfect:
‚Ä¢ ever, never (life experiences)
‚Ä¢ already, yet, just (recent actions)
‚Ä¢ recently, lately, so far (recent period)
‚Ä¢ since, for (duration)
‚Ä¢ up to now, until now (from past to present)

Present Perfect vs. Past Simple:

Present Perfect Past Simple
Time NOT specified / Still relevant Specific time in the past

(When? Not important - just the experience)		 I went to Japan in 2019.
(Specific time mentioned)
She has written five books.
(In her life - continues)		 She wrote a book last year.
(Specific time - finished)

(Ever in your life?)		 Did you see this movie yesterday?
(Yesterday specifically)

6. PRESENT PERFECT CONTINUOUS

The Purpose: Why Do We Use It?

Present Perfect Continuous emphasizes the duration and continuity of an action that started in the past and continues now, or just finished with present evidence.

Formula: have/has + been + verb-ing

I/You/We/They + have been + verb-ing
He/She/It + has been + verb-ing

Main Uses:

1. EMPHASIS ON DURATION

How long something has been happening

‚úì I've been studying English for five years.
‚úì How long have you been waiting?
‚úì She's been living in London since 2020.
‚úì They've been working on this project for months.

2. RECENT ACTIVITY with EVIDENCE

The activity just stopped and we can see the result

‚úì You look tired. Have you been working out?
‚úì It's been raining all morning. (The ground is wet)
‚úì Why are your hands dirty? Have you been gardening?
‚úì She's been crying. (Her eyes are red)

Present Perfect vs. Present Perfect Continuous:

Present Perfect Present Perfect Continuous
Focus on RESULT / COMPLETION Focus on DURATION / PROCESS
I've read three books this month.
(Completed - 3 books finished)		 I've been reading for hours.
(Duration - still in process or just finished)
She has painted the room.
(Finished - the room is painted)		 She has been painting the room.
(Continuous action - might not be finished)
They have learned 100 words.
(Achievement - exact number)		 They have been learning vocabulary.
(Ongoing activity - duration emphasized)
üí° Quick Rule:
‚Ä¢ Want to emphasize HOW MANY or WHAT RESULT? ‚Üí Use Present Perfect
‚Ä¢ Want to emphasize HOW LONG or THE ACTIVITY ITSELF? ‚Üí Use Present Perfect Continuous

7. VERBS & ADJECTIVES WITH PREPOSITIONS

In English, many verbs and adjectives are followed by specific prepositions. These combinations must be learned together as fixed expressions. Learning the correct preposition is just as important as learning the verb or adjective itself!

Key Grammar Rules:

Rule 1: Prepositions after verbs and adjectives ALWAYS have an object.
I rely on my friends. (friends = object)
She's good at mathematics. (mathematics = object)
Rule 2: When a preposition is followed by a verb, the verb is in the -ing form.
I look forward to meeting you. (NOT: to meet)
She apologized for taking the cake. (NOT: for take)
He succeeded in passing the exam. (NOT: in pass)

Complete Preposition Reference with Multiple Examples

1. SUCCEED IN
Meaning: to achieve what you were trying to do; to accomplish a goal
‚úì Do you think you'll succeed in passing your next exam?
‚úì She succeeded in getting the job despite strong competition.
‚úì They succeeded in launching their business last year.
‚úì I finally succeeded in convincing my parents.
2. OPT FOR
Meaning: to choose one thing instead of another; to make a choice
‚úì If you could improve your English by watching DVDs, by living in an English-speaking country, or by studying from books, which would you opt for?
‚úì I opted for the vegetarian meal on the plane.
‚úì Many students opt for online courses these days.
‚úì He opted for early retirement at age 55.
3. STEM FROM
Meaning: to originate from; to be caused by; to have as a source
‚úì Do your problems in English stem from poor grammar, or are there other problems?
‚úì His confidence stems from years of experience.
‚úì The word 'telephone' stems from Greek.
‚úì Most of their disagreements stem from misunderstandings.
4. LACK IN / BE LACKING IN
Meaning: to not have enough of something; to be deficient in
‚úì Do you feel you are lacking in vocabulary?
‚úì The project is lacking in creativity.
‚úì His presentation was lacking in detail.
‚úì She's not lacking in confidence!
5. BE RIDDLED WITH
Meaning: to be full of something (usually negative); to contain many instances of
‚úì Even at advanced level, some students' spoken English is riddled with errors.
‚úì The old building is riddled with cracks.
‚úì His essay was riddled with spelling mistakes.
‚úì The system is riddled with corruption.
6. DISTINGUISH FROM
Meaning: to recognize the difference between two things; to differentiate
‚úì What distinguishes your first language from English?
‚úì Can you distinguish real leather from fake?
‚úì It's hard to distinguish fact from fiction in this story.
‚úì What distinguishes humans from animals is our ability to reason.
7. APPEAL TO
Meaning: to be attractive or interesting to someone; to be liked by
‚úì What types of classroom exercises appeal to you?
‚úì This type of music doesn't appeal to me.
‚úì The idea of working from home appeals to many people.
‚úì Action movies appeal to younger audiences.
8. BOTHER WITH / BOTHER ABOUT
Meaning: to make the effort to do something; to take the time or trouble
‚úì Is pronunciation worth bothering with, or are you happy to keep your accent?
‚úì Don't bother with the dishes - I'll do them later.
‚úì Why bother about small details?
‚úì He didn't even bother with an apology.
9. BE NERVOUS ABOUT
Meaning: to feel worried or anxious about something
‚úì Are you nervous about giving presentations in English?
‚úì I'm nervous about the interview tomorrow.
‚úì She's nervous about starting her new job.
‚úì Don't be nervous about meeting my parents!
10. BENEFIT FROM
Meaning: to gain an advantage or profit from something; to be helped by
‚úì How can your vocabulary benefit from using the media?
‚úì Students benefit from regular practice.
‚úì Your health will benefit from regular exercise.
‚úì The economy has benefited from low interest rates.
11. RELY ON
Meaning: to depend on someone or something; to trust
‚úì Olga tended to rely on translation when she learned new words.
‚úì You can rely on me to help you.
‚úì Many people rely on public transportation.
‚úì Don't rely on the weather forecast too much!
12. BE SHORT OF
Meaning: to not have enough of something; to lack
‚úì Paco found he was very short of opportunities to use his English.
‚úì We're short of time, so let's hurry!
‚úì The hospital is short of staff.
‚úì I'm a bit short of cash right now.
